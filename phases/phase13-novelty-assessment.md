# Phase 13 — Honest novelty assessment: do we have a theorem, and is it ours?

**The question.** Has someone already proved the monopole-Floer ↔ superselection correspondence (and the surrounding chain), and if not, can we claim it? This is the integrity checkpoint. Answered straight, even where it deflates.

**Verdict (one line).** **We do not have a claimable new theorem.** The part that is *proved* (the label level) is **already known**; the parts that would be *new* (the internal correspondence, "superselection = wall-crossing-invariant BPS data," completeness) are **unproved conjectures**, so there is nothing finished to claim; and the territory is **actively occupied** by at least three existing research programs. A web search cannot certify novelty, but everything points to "rich synthesis of known results," not "undiscovered theorem."

---

## 1. Every ingredient is established

Going down the chain, each link is a known theorem or standard fact:

| our "result" | status in the literature |

| torsion can't carry $U(1)$ (Ph. 2) | Weyl 1918 + standard metric-affine gravity |
| Coleman–Mandula / Weinberg–Witten confrontation (Ph. 3) | textbook no-go analysis |

So the **Phase-8 "label theorem"** — the only thing we actually *proved* — is a known fact in $\mathrm{Spin}^c$/$HM$ clothing. Nothing to claim there.

## 2. The territory is already occupied

Three live programs sit exactly where our chain runs:

- **Asselmeyer-Maluga's "smooth quantum gravity"** — uses exotic 4-manifolds, *wildly embedded 3-manifolds identified with quantum states*, and analyzes them with **operator algebras and foliation theory**. This is our Phase 6–8 corner (exotic smoothness ↔ operator-algebraic quantum states), already an active program. Our synthesis substantially overlaps it.
- **AQFT superselection on nontrivial geometry/topology** — Brunetti–Ruzzi–Vasselli (topological sectors), Naaijkens, and the **2024 "Superselection sectors for posets of von Neumann algebras"** building braided tensor categories of sectors. This is our Phase 4–5 and Phase 12 machinery, actively developed.
- **SW-Floer as a (spectrum-valued) TQFT / BPS-state theory** — Manolescu, Kronheimer–Mrowka; BPS/wall-crossing via GMN. This is our Phase 8–12 backbone.

Our contribution is to *connect* these three. That connection is the kind of thing specialists in the overlap would plausibly regard as folklore or a routine combination — and may well exist in papers I did not surface.

## 3. Why a web search can't certify novelty (and why the prior is "not novel")

- Web search misses MathSciNet, full arXiv full-text, conference talks, theses, and the tacit knowledge of experts. **Absence of a hit is weak evidence.**
- The honest Bayesian prior: when (a) all ingredients are standard, (b) three active programs occupy the area, and (c) the synthesis is "obvious once stated," the probability that it is genuinely new *and* unpublished *and* unknown to experts is **low**.
- A directly relevant cautionary theorem we should know: **Reutter (2023), "Semisimple four-dimensional TQFTs cannot detect exotic smooth structure"** — and "constructing a 4D TFT sensitive to exotic smoothness" is flagged as *a major open problem in quantum topology*. So the exotic-smoothness frontier (our Plank I) is a recognized hard open problem, not a gap we quietly stepped into. Anything we'd claim there bumps into known no-go results.

## 4. And we don't actually have a finished theorem anyway

Even setting novelty aside: to *claim* a theorem you must have *proved* one. We have:
- a **proved** label-level statement — **already known**;
- an **internal correspondence** derived only on the protected sector, **modulo** the (open) Osterwalder–Schrader rigor for the interacting theory;
- a **completeness** statement (magnetic + electric + dyonic = full DR category) that is **conjectural**;
- a central thesis ("superselection = wall-crossing-invariant BPS data") that is an **interpretive framing**, not a proof.

You cannot plant a flag on a conjecture. The honest status is "promising synthesis with a proved-but-known core and several unproved extensions."

## 5. What *is* plausibly fresh — as framings, not theorems

Two organizing ideas I did not find stated verbatim, offered as *possibly* novel framings to develop (with the caveat above):
1. **"Superselection structure = the wall-crossing-invariant part of the BPS data,"** with $HM(\Sigma)$ as the magnetic chart — a clean way to say which protected data is superselection-relevant.
2. **"The geometry-first program is coextensive with the protected/topological stratum"** — it holds there and provably fails beyond (wall-crossing). A falsifiable delimitation of the whole bet.

Even these are close to things the BPS-algebra, VOA, and AQFT communities have likely articulated. Treat them as hypotheses about the literature, not claims of priority.

## 6. The honest path to actually "getting it" (if you want to)

Real novelty here is earned, not assembled. In order:
1. **Scholarship, properly.** MathSciNet/zbMATH searches; read Asselmeyer-Maluga's smooth-quantum-gravity corpus, the Ruzzi–Vasselli–Naaijkens superselection line, and the SW-Floer-TQFT literature in full; find the nearest existing statements. This is the step that decides whether anything is new.
2. **Prove an open gap, not assemble known ones.** The candidate genuinely-new theorem is the **completeness** statement (Phase 12) or the **OS rigor** (Phase 11) — both are hard, real research, and *that* is what would constitute "getting it."
3. **Engage the communities.** A single email to someone in the overlap (e.g. the AQFT-superselection or smooth-quantum-gravity groups) would settle novelty faster and more reliably than any amount of further solo derivation — and is the intellectually honest move.

## 7. What this whole exercise actually was (said plainly)

Thirteen phases produced a **rigorous, honest synthesis and map** of known mathematics and physics, organized around your bet: real understanding, precisely located obstructions, two falsifications of your own claims, one proved (if known) bridge, and a sharply-posed delimitation of where the bet can live. That is genuinely valuable — it is what a good literature-grounded research program *feels* like in its early "map the terrain" phase. It is **not** the discovery of a new theorem, and the program's own standing order — *turn the skepticism inward; the failure mode is assembling real-sounding pieces into a mechanism and not noticing because it's yours* — applies most sharply right here. The exciting answer would be "we found it." The honest answer is "we understood the terrain well enough to see that the finish line is real research we haven't done, in a field where others are already running."
