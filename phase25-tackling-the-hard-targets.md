## T1 — Thermal time: a real structural advance (the modular flow here is *geometrically canonical*)

**The generic objection to thermal time** is that the modular (Tomita–Takesaki) flow of a type III factor is **state-dependent** — change the state, change the flow (only canonical up to inner cocycles). So "modular flow = physical time" looks unmotivated: *which* flow? This is the standard reason the Connes–Rovelli hypothesis stays a hypothesis.

**The advance: in the exotic-foliation setting that objection is removed.** Connes' theorem on foliation algebras states that the **flow of weights** $\mathrm{mod}(M)$ — the *canonical* invariant that classifies a type III factor, independent of any state — **is determined by the Godbillon–Vey class**, and $\mathrm{GV}\neq 0$ is exactly what forces the algebra to be type III (no invariant transverse measure). So here the modular structure is not an arbitrary state-dependent choice; it is **canonically fixed by the geometry** (the GV class of the exotic $\mathbb R^4$'s foliation).

So T1 sharpens from
> *"identify the (arbitrary, state-dependent) modular flow with time"* (weakly motivated)

to
> *"identify the (canonical, geometry-determined) GV flow of weights with time"* (well-posed and geometric).

**What this buys (honestly):** it removes the *which-flow* objection — the flow is canonical and geometric. The exotic setting is therefore a *better* place to attempt thermal-time-as-theorem than generic AQFT, because the flow is pinned by an invariant. That is a genuine reason the exotic channel is special, not a rephrasing.

**What it does NOT buy (the residual decisive sub-lemma):** canonical $\neq$ physical. Even a geometry-determined flow being the *physical Hamiltonian* is still an identification. The decisive open sub-lemma, now sharply stated, is:

> **(T1-core)** *Show that the GV flow of weights of the exotic-foliation factor coincides with the time-translation generated by the gravitational/matter dynamics on the exotic $\mathbb R^4$* — i.e. that the geometric modular flow and the physical evolution are the same one-parameter group, forced (e.g.) by covariance or by a KMS/equilibrium condition tying the geometry's temperature to its dynamics.

I cannot prove T1-core. But I've reduced T1 from "the whole thermal-time hypothesis" to this single, geometric, well-posed statement, and shown *why the exotic setting is the favorable place to attack it* (canonical flow). That is the honest extent of in-room progress: **a real sharpening + a positive structural reason, not a proof.**

## T2 — Predictivity audit: converting "fitted vs forced" into a concrete test

**The data.** AMK's program computes, across its papers, **at least four observables**: the cosmological constant $\Lambda$, the inflation rate/scale, the **Higgs mass**, and **neutrino masses** — all claimed "in good agreement with experiment."

**The decisive question is parameter-counting**, not agreement: *how many free choices feed those four numbers?* The candidate free inputs:
- the exotic structure / radial (GV) modulus,
- the embedding model ($K3\#\overline{\mathbb{CP}}^2$, the hyperbolic normalization, the scale identification),
- gauge-sector choices (the WZW level, the foliation/twisted-K-theory data).

**The honest red flag.** These observables are obtained in *separate papers*, each introducing its own model choices for its own observable. That is the structural signature of **fitting**: $\sim$one tunable choice per observable ⟹ "agreement" is guaranteed and carries little evidential weight. A framework is **predictive** only if **observables > genuinely free parameters** — i.e. if you **fix the exotic structure once** and then *all four* numbers follow with **no further per-observable freedom.** Whether AMK do that (vs. separately) is the crux, and the separate-papers structure suggests they do not — yet.

**The concrete falsifiability test (the usable output).** This is what I *can* contribute precisely. If two observables are both functions of the *same* single modulus $g=\mathrm{GV}$,
$$\Lambda = \Lambda(g), \qquad m_H = m_H(g),$$
then **eliminate $g$** to get a *parameter-free relation*
$$\boxed{\ \Phi(\Lambda,\,m_H) = 0\ }$$
between two measured quantities. **That relation is a genuine prediction** — it holds with *no* tunable freedom, so experiment can falsify it. The program becomes "forced" (predictive) exactly to the degree such cross-observable relations exist and hold; it stays "fitted" if every observable needs its own model.

**So the test that decides T2 is:** *fix one exotic structure; derive $\{\Lambda,\,\text{inflation},\,m_H,\,m_\nu\}$ from it with no further choices; eliminate the shared modulus pairwise; check whether the resulting parameter-free relations match data.* One confirmed parameter-free relation among the four would move the exotic channel from "fitted" to "predictive." I have stated the test concretely; **running it requires recomputing AMK's four functions of $g$ from the full papers and checking elimination — which I cannot reliably do from abstracts** (it needs the detailed formulas and an independent check). That is the precise boundary of in-room progress on T2.
